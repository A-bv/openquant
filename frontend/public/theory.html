did the theory hold?

We tested the valuation theory on 10 years of real returns.

The course says a reverse-DCF reveals whether a stock is cheap or expensive. So we ran it on 50 big US stocks as of January 2014, then checked what actually happened by January 2024.

The honest answer: it barely worked, and not the way you would think.

44%
of the stocks it called cheap actually beat the market. Worse than a coin flip.
backwards
the stocks it called expensive earned more (13.6%/yr) than the cheap ones (12.3%/yr).
~4%
of who won vs lost is explained by the model (R² = 0.04). Almost none.

Called "fairly priced" (2 stocks)8.8%/yr

If the theory worked, the blue "undervalued" bar should be the tallest. It isn't.

That is exactly the point. A valuation is not a crystal ball. It tells you the bet baked into a price (the growth the market is already paying for), not what the stock will do next. The future depends on a thousand things a model cannot see.

This is why OpenQuant never says "this stock is worth $X." It shows you the bet, with its honest limits, and lets you decide whether you believe it.

Source: a reverse-DCF run on 50 large S&P 500 names, as of 31 Jan 2014, compared to realized total returns through Jan 2024. 33 had enough filing history to value; the rest the model could not touch. Full results in backtest/.
