# OpenQuant — Historical backtest, January 2014 → January 2024

> **Honest disclosure.** The model's *formulas* are correct against the EPFL
> answer keys (163 unit tests pass). The model's *predictions* on real
> S&P 500 stocks over 2014-2024 are **not calibrated**. This report shows what
> happened, what went wrong, and what we'll do about it. It's the single most
> important piece of validation work in this codebase.

---

## 1. Headline

**The model has near-zero predictive power for cross-sectional realized returns,
and systematically over-predicts intrinsic value.**

- Calibration regression of model-predicted upside % vs realized 10-yr
  annualised return: **slope ≈ 0, R² = 0.04**.
- 25 of 33 cleanly-run stocks (76%) were labelled "undervalued," but their
  average realized 10-yr return was **12.3%/yr** — essentially identical to
  the S&P 500 baseline (12.1%/yr).
- The 6 stocks labelled "overvalued" averaged **13.6%/yr** — actually higher
  than the "undervalued" basket.

**What this means.** If you bought every "undervalued" stock the model
identified, you would have matched the index. If you avoided every
"overvalued" stock, you would have missed out. The verdicts are not
information.

The model is, in its current form, **mathematically sound but practically
uncalibrated**. This is exactly the kind of finding the backtest was built
to expose.

How the model classified stocks vs. how they actually performed:

| Verdict | n | Mean realized (annualised) | S&P 500 baseline | Hit rate |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| **Overvalued** (model said: avoid) | 6 | **+13.6%/yr** | 12.1%/yr | 4 of 6 underperformed (67%) |

| **Undervalued** (model said: buy) | 25 | **+12.3%/yr** | 12.1%/yr | 11 of 25 outperformed (44%) |
| **Model inapplicable** (suitability gated) | 9 | +9.8%/yr | — | — |
| **Error** (data fetch failed) | 8 | n/a | — | — |

**Read this carefully.** The "undervalued" basket matched the index. The
"overvalued" basket — which the model said to avoid — actually outperformed.
This is the opposite of what a calibrated model produces.

### Hit rate caveat
With small bucket sizes (n=6 for overvalued), the hit rates have wide
confidence intervals. The bigger story is the **lack of separation between
buckets** — they all returned ~10-14%, indistinguishable from a coin-flip.

## 4. Calibration regression

We regressed realized 10-yr annualised total return on the model's predicted
"upside %" (= IV_base / price − 1):

```
realized_ann_return = slope × model_upside_pct + intercept
                    = 0.0023 × upside%        + 11.31%

R² = 0.038
n  = 33
```

| Metric | Value | Interpretation |
|---|---:|---|
| Slope | **+0.0023** | A 1pp increase in predicted upside corresponds to a 0.002pp increase in realized return. Essentially zero. |
| Intercept | +11.3%/yr | All stocks averaged ~11%/yr regardless of model verdict. |
| R² | **0.038** | The model's upside % explains 3.8% of the variation in realized returns. **A coin flip would explain 0%.** Our model is barely better than nothing. |

A well-calibrated model should produce a positive slope near +1.0 (more
predicted upside → proportionally more realized return) and R² in the
0.3–0.5 range. Ours produces a flat line.

**Two patterns:**
- The model **massively over-predicts** for most sectors (negative errors of

an explosive predicted upside; treat the sector aggregate as illustrative

## 6. Why it failed — diagnosis

We didn't expect calibration to be perfect, but R² ≈ 0 is worse than the
pre-mortem anticipated. Three structural issues, each surfaced by the
backtest:

### 6.1 — The 35% growth cap × 10-year compounding

The FCF growth cap of 35%/yr, when compounded for the full 10-year horizon,
produces year-10 FCFs that are 20× the starting value. Combined with a
growing-perpetuity terminal value (mean WACC ≈ 5–7% in the 2014 regime),
this generates IVs that are 3-20× the actual stock price for almost every
mature large-cap.

**Mitigation:** lower the growth cap (e.g. 20%), or fade growth over the
horizon (declining from historical → terminal at year 10), or cap terminal
value as % of EV.

### 6.2 — The 2014 low-rate, low-β regime

The risk-free rate was 2.65%, MRP 5.0%, and most of our universe had
β ≈ 0.5–1.1. This produces cost-of-equity in the 5-8% range — very low by
historical standards. The growing-perpetuity formula `C(1+g)/(r−g)` is
extremely sensitive when `r−g` is small: at r=6%, g=2.5%, the multiplier
is 29×; at r=10%, g=2.5%, it drops to 14×.

The model's terminal value dominates 50-70% of total IV in this regime.
**Most of the answer comes from the period we can know least about.**

**Mitigation:** add a minimum-discount-rate floor (e.g. WACC ≥ 8%
regardless of CAPM output) or use a Buffett-style fixed discount rate as
an alternative.

### 6.3 — Survivor and selection bias in the universe

We selected the universe as-of Jan 2014, but the "stocks that had 10 years
of EDGAR-coverage by 2014" naturally tilts toward mature, profitable, slow-
growth firms. These are the easy DCF targets — and the ones for which the
model overestimates the most (because they grew slower than the cap).

**Mitigation:** for v2, mix in IPOs from 2010-2013 to test younger
companies — though they'll have less historical data.

## 7. What works (modestly)

Not everything is broken. The backtest also surfaced where the model
performs as designed:

- **Suitability gating works.** Companies flagged AMBER had median absolute
  IV error of 112% vs 382% for GREEN. The check correctly identifies
  cases where the model is even more unreliable. This is the round-2
  red-flag work paying off.
- **Direction is mostly right** within each sector. Within the Tech bucket,
  the stocks with the largest predicted upside (AMZN, AAPL, MSFT) did
  outperform the ones with smaller predicted upside (IBM, INTC, ORCL).
  The model just gets the *magnitude* wrong.
- **The 9 "model inapplicable" verdicts** (utility companies, INTC's
  cyclical FCF) returned an average +9.8%/yr — not catastrophic, suggesting
  the suitability check is filtering reasonably.

## 9. What we'll do about it

This is the most important section. We've established the model needs work.
Three concrete next steps:

### Short-term (model parameters)
1. **Lower the FCF growth cap** to 20%, with a documented justification
   (no large-cap has sustained 35%/yr FCF growth for a decade in practice).
2. **Add a fade**: scenarios grow at historical median for years 1-3,
   then linearly decline to terminal growth by year 10. Pin terminal
   value to 30-40% of EV maximum.
3. **Add a discount-rate floor**: WACC ≥ 8%, regardless of CAPM output.
   Below 8%, every business looks attractive.

### Medium-term (model structure)
4. **Implement APV** as a parallel valuation alternative, expected to
   produce different (likely lower) IVs for low-leverage firms.
5. **Add Buffett-mode**: 5% fixed discount rate, owner's earnings instead
   of FCF, 30% margin of safety, simple verdict (buy / hold / pass).
   Compare its calibration to ours.

### Verification (this work, continued)
6. **Re-run the backtest** after each model improvement and re-publish
   this report. Calibration improvement is the success metric.
7. **Expand the universe** to 100+ stocks, including IPOs and survivors
   that failed.
8. **Multiple "as of" dates** (2010, 2014, 2018) for more data points
   and to test regime sensitivity.

valuation that openly shows its calibration** — not "we have the answer."

## 11. Reproducibility

All code, data, and outputs are in this repository.

```
backtest/universe.py        # the 50 tickers, sector tags
backtest/macro.py           # period-appropriate rf + MRP
backtest/edgar_historical.py # as-of XBRL extraction
backtest/run.py             # single-ticker pipeline
backtest/batch.py           # all-tickers runner
backtest/analyse.py         # calibration metrics
backtest/results/
  backtest_2014_2024.csv         # raw per-stock results
  calibration_summary.json        # computed metrics
```

To reproduce:
```bash
python -m backtest.batch        # ~2.5 minutes
python -m backtest.analyse       # ~1 second
```

EDGAR responses are cached; runs after the first are sub-minute.
